project overview

i've designed a dress that incorporates 3D printed elements and servo-controlled petals. the dress consists of a 3D printed top and a skirt with interactive flower petals. the flower petals will be controlled by servo motors, creating movement and interactivity in the garment.

2. servo motors and initial cardboard petals

i designed the petal mechanism in fusion 360 and then 3d printed them. the petals need to be attached to the servo motors in a way that allows them to move naturally. i created a design with attachment points that can connect to the servo motor arm as well as the petal.

initial cardboard belt design

fusion belt design

for my first iteration of the belt, i considered laser cutting cardboard to create a flexible structure that could hold the servo motors. i designed a pattern that would allow for movement while maintaining structural integrity. however, after testing this approach, i found that the cardboard was too stiff for comfortable wear and didn't conform well to body movements.

3d printed belt fabric

i then 3d printed flexible fabric for a belt to hold the servo motors. this offered better flexibility while still providing the necessary support for the electronics. i had to hand sew three of these fabric pieces together for the full belt.

3. making all the servos move

then, i tested the servo motors individually first to ensure each one functioned correctly since i had 5 servo motors all connecting to one esp32. after confirming individual functionality, i connected all five servos and programmed them to work in coordination. i added my 3d printed servo attachments and some cardboard petals to the belt.

7. mvp0 done (skirt and plastic petals)

for my minimum viable product (mvp0), i used tape to temporarily assemble the skirt components. this allowed me to test the overall functionality and appearance before committing to more permanent construction methods.

the mvp0 demonstrated that the concept was viable and that i could successfully integrate electronics with fabric

i was able to control all five petals with synchronized movements, creating an interactive garment

8. added remote control

to make the dress interactive, i added a remote control system that allows the wearer to control the movement of the petals.

9. switched to ribbon cable and soldering

to make the electronics more reliable and compact, i switched from using individual wires to ribbon cables and soldered the connections.

ribbon cable connections

testing the ribbon cable connections

10. switched to xiao esp32c3

to make the electronics more compact, i switched from using the larger esp32 to the smaller xiao esp32c3 microcontroller.

soldered xiao esp32c3

the xiao esp32c3 is much smaller than the standard esp32, making it ideal for wearable projects where space and weight are important considerations

11. making the actual dress

once all of the electronics were set up, i started working on the actual dress. i chose three types of fabric for different parts of the dress:

  • a satin outer layer for aesthetics
  • a canvas material for structure
  • a stretchy mesh for the skirt

i began by making the corset. first, i plastic wrapped and taped the mannequin to create a form-fitting template. i then used that as a guide for cutting my fabric pieces. after cutting the pieces, i glued the satin on top of the canvas for each section and then sewed them all together. i still need to clean up the edges and figure out the back closure.

next steps

for MVP0, i'm aiming to get an initial iteration of the petals on the skirt as well as the 3d printed top done. i will also sew a small scaled-down version of the skirt to ensure my process can be used for the final skirt. i also included the initial fabrics that i'll be using for MVP0. once i've gotten the electronics to work properly, i will use a different material to make the dress more sturdy.
