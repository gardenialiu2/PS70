project overview

i'm designing a dress that incorporates 3D printed elements and servo-controlled petals. the dress consists of a 3D printed top and a skirt with interactive flower petals. the flower petals will be controlled by servo motors, creating movement and interactivity in the garment.

documentation

1. initial designs

i began by sketching the overall dress design, including the 3D printed top and the interactive skirt with servo-controlled petals. i designed the petal mechanism in fusion 360, creating attachment points that could connect to both the servo motor arm and the petal itself.

2. belt, servo motors, and initial cardboard petals

i designed the petal mechanism in fusion 360 and then 3d printed them. the petals need to be attached to the servo motors in a way that allows them to move naturally. i created a design with attachment points that can connect to the servo motor arm as well as the petal. i tested the servo motors individually first to ensure each one functioned correctly since i had 5 servo motors all connecting to one esp32. after confirming individual functionality, i connected all five servos and programmed them to work in coordination.

for my first iteration of the belt, i considered laser cutting cardboard to create a flexible structure that could hold the servo motors. i designed a pattern that would allow for movement while maintaining structural integrity. however, after testing this approach, i found that the cardboard was too stiff for comfortable wear and didn't conform well to body movements.

i then 3d printed flexible fabric for a belt to hold the servo motors, which offered better flexibility while still providing necessary support for the electronics. i hand-sewed three fabric pieces together to make the strip long enough for a belt.

3. initial skirt and petals

to test my design concept, i created a proof-of-concept skirt to visualize how the final piece would look. i learned to use a sewing machine for this project, which was a valuable skill for creating both the prototype and final garment

after testing individual servos, i connected them all to work together. the coordination of all five servos was essential for creating synchronized petal movements. this testing phase helped me refine the control software for the final design.

4. plastic petals and remote control

the plastic petals were more durable and consistent than the cardboard ones, allowing for more reliable movement and a more polished appearance. to make the dress interactive, i added a remote control system that allows the wearer to control the movement of the petals. details about the remote control can be found on my week 7 page. the completed belt assembly integrated the electronics with the 3D printed fabric, allowing it to conform to the body while supporting the weight of the components.

for my minimum viable product (mvp0), i used tape to temporarily assemble the skirt components. this allowed me to test the overall functionality and appearance before committing to more permanent construction methods. the mvp0 demonstrated that the concept was viable and that i could successfully integrate electronics with fabric.

5. esp32 to xiao and ribbon cable

to make the electronics more reliable and compact, i switched from using individual wires to ribbon cables and soldered the connections. I also switched from using the larger esp32 to the smaller xiao esp32c3 microcontroller. i was able to control all five petals with synchronized movements, creating an interactive garment. switching to ribbon cables made the wiring more organized and reliable, while also making the dress more comfortable to wear. the xiao esp32c3 is much smaller than the standard esp32, making it ideal for wearable projects where space and weight are important considerations.

6. the actual dress

once all of the electronics were set up, i started working on the actual dress. i chose three types of fabric for different parts of the dress:

  • a satin outer layer for aesthetics
  • a canvas material for structure
  • a stretchy mesh for the skirt

i began by making the corset. first, i plastic wrapped and taped the mannequin to create a form-fitting template. i then used that as a guide for cutting my fabric pieces. after cutting the pieces, i glued the satin on top of the canvas for each section and then sewed them all together. i still need to clean up the edges and figure out the back closure.

next up

to completely finish the dress, i need to sew the skirt, corset, and petal coverings. i may also make the remote control a little more visually appealing. the electronic components of the project are already assembled and tested. i also need to incorporate a battery pack and finalize the wiring harness.
