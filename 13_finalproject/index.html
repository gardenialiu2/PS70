project overview

hi! i'm named after a gardenia flower so i decided to design a gardenia-inspired dress that incorporates 3d printed elements and servo-controlled petals. the dress consists of a satin corset top, a mesh maxi skirt, and an interactive flower petal top skirt layer. the servo motors within the flower petals are remote controlled using esp32now, a xiao, and an esp32. the petals move in and out like a flower blooming and closing. the petals can also move in a wave-like motion too. each petal is made from multiple layers of fabric (canvas for structure and satin on top), and they are attached over a servo belt so that the electronics are hidden inside the flower petals. full process video:

1. initial designs

2. belt, servo motors, and initial cardboard petals

i designed the petal mechanism in fusion 360 and then 3d printed the mechanical parts that connect to the servo horns. the petals needed to attach to the servo motors in a way that allowed them to move naturally, so i built a small linkage with attachment points for both the servo arm and the petal. i tested each of the five servo motors individually to confirm they worked before wiring them all to a single esp32 and programming coordinated motion patterns. to mount everything on the body, i 3d printed flexible belt “fabric” that could hold the servos while still bending around the waist, and then hand-sewed three pieces of this printed fabric together to make the belt long enough.

3. initial skirt and petals

to test my design concept, i created a proof-of-concept skirt to visualize how the final piece would look and used it as a playground for hanging early petals. i learned to use a sewing machine for this project, which was a valuable skill for both the prototype and final garment. i first tested the servos with simple cardboard petals, starting with one servo at a time and then scaling up to multiple servos working together. once all five servos were connected, i refined the code so the petals opened in a coordinated, flower-like motion rather than moving randomly.

4. plastic petals and remote control

i then switched to 3d printing the petals since plastic petals were more durable and consistent than the cardboard ones. to make the dress interactive, i added a remote control system that lets the wearer trigger different petal movement patterns; the input details and testing are documented on my week 7 page. for my minimum viable product (mvp0), i used tape to temporarily assemble the skirt components, just to verify that the electronics, belt, and petals all worked together on the body. this taped-together version confirmed that the concept was viable and that the electronics and fabric could be integrated into a wearable piece. once that was done, i replaced the tape by hand-sewing the servos to the belt.

5. esp32 to xiao and ribbon cable

all of the servo motors were connected to an esp32. to make the electronics more reliable and compact, i switched from using individual jumper wires to ribbon cables and soldered the connections. i also swapped the larger esp32 for the smaller xiao esp32c3 microcontroller, which freed up space and made the belt more comfortable. with the new wiring, i could still control all five petals with synchronized movements, but the wiring was much cleaner and less likely to snag on fabric. the ribbon cables were sewn into place and routed along the belt so that they stayed flat against the body, and the xiao esp32c3 sat neatly with the rest of the electronics.

6. the actual dress

once all of the electronics were set up, i started working on the actual dress. i chose a satin outer layer for aesthetics, a canvas layer for structure, and a stretchy mesh layer for the skirt and back ties so the dress could move and still be adjustable. to build the corset, i plastic wrapped and taped the mannequin to create a form-fitting template, then used that as a guide for cutting my fabric pieces. i glued satin on top of the canvas panels and sewed them together on the machine to create the base corset, then cleaned up the edges and finished the seams so it would be comfortable and neat. instead of leaving the back open, i added some strips of mesh material as a tie so the corset could be tightened and adjusted. after finishing the corset base, i moved on to the petals that sit over the servo belt: each petal has a structural canvas layer and a satin top layer, sewn together on the machine for clean edges. i used a combination of hot glue and hand sewing to attach these petals to the belt that holds all the servo motors, arranging them into two overlapping layers to create a fuller flower effect around the waist. as a final step, i hid all of the motors inside the volume of the petals so that the mechanics are invisible when the dress is worn, and attached the finished petal belt securely to the corset so the petals look like they grow directly out of the bodice. i also hid the portable charger within one of the petals.

7. code files

the code that controls the petals and remote lives in separate files: petal_control_code.ino (main servo and petal motion code) and remote_receiver_code.ino (remote control and input handling).
