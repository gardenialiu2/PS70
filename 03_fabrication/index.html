week 3: hand tools and fabrication

final kinetic sculpture (spinning waterlily)

process

i went through many phases of brainstorming for this before i finally decided on this current iteration. for this final sculpture, i modeled it after a flower windmill, where spinning would create an optical illusion that the flower was full.

this sculpture has a few main parts: the platform that the motor sits on, the rod that the motor connects to, and the petals of the flower. i decided to keep the base pretty simple. i cut out a box that fits the motor and added additional pieces to make it look like the lily pad and leaves of a waterlily. for the motor and rod connection, i opted for a triangular stand that would fold up around the rod. as for the flower, i made cut out thin petals and a pistil to fit around the rod.

fusion 360 design of the kinetic sculpture

the base is just a box that fits the protruding parts of the motor. this box is attached to a lilypad cutout. then, i used a triangular structure with 3 pieces that fold upwards to secure the rod to the motor. the petals of the flower have a hole cut out in the center so that the rod can fit through. these petal pieces are scored to create a curved shape.

previous idea

i wanted to create a flower that could open and close. i began by sketching out some ideas for the flower shape and how it should move. my brainstorming is shown below:

then, i designed the component in fusion 360 to be laser cut. i wanted a simple shape to latch onto the spinning part of the motor, so i measured the dimensions of the shape and put that in fusion 360. since i was unsure how the cardboard kerf would affect the final dimensions and i needed the cutout to fit very tightly, i laser cut a couple prototypes to check the dimensions.

i also started out with 3mm cardboard but soon i realized that the laser would burn through part of the cardboard hole and the cardboard was too flimsy, so i opted for 6mm cardboard instead.

once i had some prototypes, i played around with how the petal should move. i decided to use a thin wire to connect the petal to the spinning part of the motor. i then added a cutout in the base of the petal's stem so that the wire could fit through.

i eventually also cut out multiple metals to create the flower, where the goal was to have 2 petals be connected to the motor and move back and forth. by positioning these petals over the other petals, i hoped that the other petals would also move back and forth, simulating how the whole flower would open and close. however, i ran into some issues with the cardboard being too sturdy for the wire to move it, as shown in the video on the right.

learnings & challenges

  • the motor had many different protruding parts => print multiple iterations
  • kerf affected the final dimensions => measure multiple times
  • 3mm cardboard was too thin + laser burned through => use 6mm cardboard
